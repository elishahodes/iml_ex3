Question 3 – Convergence rate (||w|| vs iteration) for all learning rates

L2 module:
- eta=1: the norm stays exactly constant. The L2 gradient is 2w, so the update is
  w_new = w - 1 * 2w = -w. Each iteration flips the sign of w without changing its magnitude,
  so the weights oscillate between +w0 and -w0 forever. The contraction factor |1 - 2*eta|
  equals exactly 1, giving neither convergence nor divergence — just perpetual oscillation.
- eta=0.1 and eta=0.01: both converge to 0. eta=0.1 converges faster (fewer iterations needed)
  because it takes larger, yet still stable, steps. Each iteration contracts the norm by the
  factor |1 - 2*eta|: for eta=0.1 the factor is 0.8, for eta=0.01 it is 0.98 — so eta=0.1 is
  geometrically faster.
- eta=0.001: converges slowly (contraction factor 0.998), reaching a relatively large residual
  after 1000 iterations.

L1 module:
- eta=0.001: decreases very slowly; 1000 steps of size 0.001 move each coordinate by at most 1
  unit, leaving ||w|| still large.
- eta=0.01: steadily decreases, reaching a small value near zero.
- eta=0.1: decreases quickly but then oscillates around a small residual.
- eta=1: oscillates with large amplitude — each step of size 1 overshoots zero repeatedly,
  preventing convergence.

General principle: a learning rate that is too large causes oscillation or divergence; one that
is too small converges but very slowly. There is an optimal range that balances speed and stability.
the lower the learning rate, the closer we oscillate around the minimum.
