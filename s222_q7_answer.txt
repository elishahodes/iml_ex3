Section 2.2.2 Question 7 – Confusion matrix analysis

Two most common confusions (true → predicted):
  1. 7 → 9: digit 7 predicted as 9. These two digits share a similar angular stroke at the top;
     some handwritten 7s can resemble 9s especially when the top is closed.
  2. 9 → 4: digit 9 predicted as 4. Both digits have a closed loop at the top and a descending
     stroke; certain handwriting styles make them hard to distinguish.

Three least common confusions (true → predicted):
  These are pairs (true, predicted) that essentially never co-occur. For example (0, 1), (0, 2),
  (0, 9) — zero is rarely mistaken for one, two, or nine. This makes sense: the digit 0 is a
  closed oval and is visually very distinct from digits 1 and 2. The network almost never
  confuses these visually dissimilar classes.

Do these results make sense?
  Yes. The most common confusions involve pairs of digits that share visual features
  (similar strokes, angles, loops), which even humans sometimes confuse in handwriting.
  The least common confusions are between visually very different digits where even
  noisy or unusual handwriting is unlikely to create ambiguity.
