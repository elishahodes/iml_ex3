Question 6 – Exponential decay vs fixed LR for L1; lowest L1 norm achieved

Lowest L1 norm achieved with exponential decay:
  gamma=0.95 and gamma=0.99 both achieve L1 loss ≈ 0 (convergence to the true minimum w=0).

Comparison with fixed learning rate:
  With a fixed learning rate, the algorithm cannot converge to exactly w=0 for the L1 objective.
  The best fixed LR (eta=0.01) achieved loss ~0.008. The fundamental issue is that the L1
  gradient is always ±1 in each direction, so any positive fixed step causes oscillation around
  zero.

  With exponential decay, eta_t = eta * gamma^t → 0 as t → ∞. This means:
  - Initially, large steps enable fast progress toward the minimum.
  - Later, steps become small enough to resolve the fine structure near zero and "land" on it.
  - The decreasing step size suppresses the oscillation that plagues the fixed-rate algorithm.

Why the difference:
  A necessary condition for convergence of subgradient methods on non-smooth objectives is that
  the step sizes satisfy sum(eta_t) = ∞ (to reach the minimum) and sum(eta_t^2) < ∞ (to ensure
  convergence rather than oscillation). Exponential decay satisfies the second condition (the
  geometric series is summable), allowing convergence to the true minimum. A fixed learning rate
  satisfies neither (sum diverges, but sum of squares also diverges), causing oscillation.
