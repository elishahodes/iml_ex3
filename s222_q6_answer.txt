Section 2.2.2 Question 6 – MNIST convergence process (SGD)

Loss vs iteration:
  The loss drops rapidly in the first few hundred iterations (SGD makes fast initial progress
  because each batch provides a noisy but useful gradient estimate), then levels off and
  decreases more slowly. Unlike GD, the curve is noisy/jagged due to the stochastic nature
  of batch sampling — each iteration uses a different random mini-batch, so the loss estimate
  fluctuates around the true loss.

Gradient norm vs iteration:
  The gradient norm is large at the start and generally trends downward as the network
  approaches a good solution. Due to the mini-batch noise, the norm does not smoothly
  decrease — it fluctuates throughout training. This is expected: SGD never converges in
  the classical GD sense; it converges in expectation and then wanders in a neighborhood
  of the optimum.

The noisy convergence is the key characteristic of SGD that distinguishes it from GD.
Despite the noise, SGD reaches a good solution much faster (in wall-clock time) than GD
on large datasets, because it processes small batches rather than the full dataset each step.
